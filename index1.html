Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar

Chandrasekhar was an Indian physicist who made significant contributions to the structure of stars and black holes.

Here's a time line of "Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar" life:

  • 1910 -19 October 1910 Lahore, Punjab, British India (modern Punjab, Pakistan)
  • 1916-1918 - The family moved from Lahore to Allahabad in 1916, and finally settled in Madras in 1918.
  • 1933 - Chandrasekhar was awarded his PhD degree at Cambridge in the summer of 1933, with a thesis on rotating self-gravitating polytrophic. He was the first Indian to be awarded a PhD in astrophysics.
  • 1935 - In 1935, Chandrasekhar was invited by the director of the Harvard Observatory, Harlow Shapely, to be a visiting lecturer in theoretical astrophysics for a three-month period. He traveled to the United States in December. During his visit to Harvard, Chandrasekhar greatly impressed Shaley, but declined his offer of a Harvard research fellowship. At the same time, Chandrasekhar met Gerard Kuiper, a noted Dutch astrophysical observational who was then a leading authority on white dwarfs.
  • 1943 -During World War II, Chandrasekhar worked at the Ballistic Research Laboratory at the Aberdeen Proving Ground in Maryland. While there, he worked on problems of ballistics, he expertise in hydrodynamics led Robert Oppenheimer to invite him to join the Manhattan Project at Los Alamos, but delays in the processing of his security clearance prevented him from contributing to the project. It has been rumoured that he visited the Calutron project.
  • Personal life - Chandrasekhar was the nephew of C. V. Raman, who was awarded the Nobel Prize for Physics in 1930. Chandrasekhar married "Lalitha Doraiswamy" in September 1936. He met her as a fellow student at Presidency College. He became a citizen of the U.S. in 1953. Many considered him as warm, positive, generous, unassuming, meticulous, and open to debate, while some others as private, intimidating, impatient and stubborn regarding non-scientific matters, and unforgiving to those who ridiculed his work. Chandrasekhar was a vegetarian. Chandrasekhar died of a heart attack at the University ogitf Chicago Hospital in 1995, having survived a prior heart attack in 1975. He was survived by his wife, who died on 2 September 2013 at the age of 102. She was a serious student of literature and western classical music.
  • Religious beliefs - Chandrasekhar was a Hindu by birth, but he did not practice any religion. He was an atheist and did not believe in the existence of God. He was known to be a rationalist and a skeptic, often questioning religious beliefs and practices. Once when involved in a discussion about the Bhagavad Gita, Chandrasekhar said: "I should like to preface my remarks with a personal statement in order that my later remarks will not be misunderstood. I consider myself an atheist".
  • Other activities - Chandrasekhar published his first paper on the stability of white dwarfs, which led to the discovery of the Chandrasekhar limit. This limit is the maximum mass (approximately 1.4 solar masses) that a white dwarf star can have before it collapses under its own gravity, leading to either a supernova or the formation of a neutron star or black hole. From 1952 to 1971 Chandrasekhar was editor of The Astrophysical Journal. When Eugene Parker submitted a paper on his discovery of solar wind in 1957, two eminent reviewers rejected the paper. However, since Chandra as an editor could not find any mathematical flaws in Parker's work, he went ahead and published the paper in 1958. During the years 1990 to 1995, Chandrasekhar worked on a project devoted to explaining the detailed geometric arguments in Sir Isaac Newton's Philosophiae, Naturals, Principia, Mathematica using the language and methods of ordinary calculus. The effort resulted in the book Newton's Principia for the Common Reader, published in 1995. Chandrasekhar also worked on collision of gravitational waves, and algebraically special perturbations.
  • 1983 - Chandrasekhar was awarded half of the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1983 for his studies on the physical processes important to the structure and evolution of stars. Chandrasekhar accepted this honour, but was upset the citation mentioned only his earliest work, seeing it as a denigration of a lifetime's achievement.
  • 1995 - Chandrasekhar died of a heart attack at the University of Chicago Hospital in 1995, having survived a prior heart attack in 1975.
